                                        Abstract

This thesis investigates one-class anomaly detection in brain magnetic resonance imag-
ing (MRI) on the BMAD brain MRI benchmark derived from BraTS. The problem is
motivated by a common medical-imaging setting: normal anatomy can be collected and
described more reliably than the full range of possible pathologies. The work therefore
studies whether a model trained only on normal slices can identify tumour-containing
slices and produce useful pixel-level anomaly maps. In particular, it explores the com-
bination of DINOv3 visual representations with an objective adapted from Class Anchor
Margin (CAM) loss, using anchors as prototypes of normal brain structure.
    The proposed models use DINOv3 features, anchors obtained from normal embed-
dings, and a two-stage training procedure that combines anchor-distance, reconstruction,
and divergence-based scores. The experiments follow the evolution of the project: ini-
tial anchor-construction strategies, global centroid-anchor models, shared patch-anchor
models, and location-aware patch-centroid models. Across these families, the best trained
variants reach fused image-level AUROC values of approximately 0.83 and pixel-level
AUROC values around 0.93. A training-free PatchCore baseline using frozen DINOv3
features remains the strongest reference result, with 0.8837 image AUROC and 0.9612
pixel AUROC.
    The comparison ranks the methods and clarifies the behaviour of class-anchor learning
in a one-class medical setting. The results show that anchor construction, spatial gran-
ularity, reconstruction quality, and score fusion all have a visible effect on performance.
Reconstruction is a useful auxiliary signal for localisation, while divergence is less con-
sistent and works best when interpreted as an indirect cue. Overall, the thesis presents
class-anchor learning as an interpretable direction for one-class MRI anomaly detection,
clarifies where it helps, and identifies the design choices that most strongly shape its
results.

Chapter 1

Introduction

1.1     Overview and Scope
    This thesis lies at the intersection of self-supervised representation learning and un-
supervised medical anomaly detection. Its central question is whether the dense visual
representations produced by a modern self-supervised vision backbone, namely DINOv3
[1], can be combined with a class-anchor metric-learning objective [2] to perform one-
class anomaly detection on brain magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). The work is framed
as a systematic empirical study: we adapt a supervised objective to a one-class setting,
evaluate the resulting models against a strong frozen-feature PatchCore [3] reference, and
analyse how the anchor, reconstruction, divergence, and patch-based variants contribute
to the final behaviour.


1.2     Anomaly Detection in Medical Imaging
    Anomaly detection is the task of identifying samples that deviate significantly from
an expected, normal distribution [4]. In medical imaging, an anomaly typically corre-
sponds to an abnormal structure, lesion, or pattern that may indicate the presence of a
disease or other pathological condition [5, 6]. A defining characteristic of this domain
is a strong asymmetry between the two classes: normal anatomy is comparatively well
defined and abundant, whereas abnormalities are heterogeneous, individually rare, and
effectively open-ended. It is implausible to assemble a training set that covers every
possible pathology, particularly for rare or previously unseen conditions.
    This asymmetry has direct methodological consequences. A conventional supervised
classifier, trained to distinguish a fixed set of disease categories, is poorly suited to a
problem whose negative class cannot be enumerated and tends to fail on abnormalities
it has never observed during training. The objective of medical anomaly detection is to
raise an alert whenever an image departs from the learned notion of normality; closed-set


                                            6
disease labels are outside this formulation [4, 5, 6].


1.2.1    The One-Class Formulation
    These considerations motivate the one-class formulation adopted throughout this work.
Under this paradigm, the model is trained exclusively on normal images and learns a rep-
resentation of the normal distribution; at inference time, a sample is assigned an anomaly
score that quantifies its deviation from that distribution. This setting reflects the support-
estimation view of one-class learning [7] and the realistic situation in clinical practice,
where disease-free examples are relatively easy to obtain while a comprehensive collection
of abnormal examples is not. We instantiate the problem on the brain MRI benchmark
of BMAD [5], which is reorganised from the BraTS2021 tumour segmentation dataset [8]
and supports both image-level detection and pixel-level localisation of anomalies.


1.3     Motivation and Research Question
    Two recent lines of work motivate the approach studied here. First, large self-supervised
models such as DINOv3 [1] produce general-purpose dense features that rival or surpass
specialised and supervised models across a wide range of dense prediction tasks. This
makes them attractive as a backbone for medical anomaly detection, where annotated
data is scarce [9, 10]. Second, the Class Anchor Margin (CAM) loss [2] offers a mecha-
nism for shaping an embedding space into compact, well-separated clusters by attracting
samples toward learnable anchors, which suggests a natural way to model the normal
distribution.
    Combining these ideas yields the hypothesis investigated in this work: if representative
“basal” prototypes of the normal data are obtained as cluster centroids and used as
anchors, and if normal images are encouraged to lie close to these anchors during training,
then at test time a normal sample should fall near some anchor while an anomalous sample
should lie far from every anchor. The distance to the nearest anchor then serves as an
anomaly score. The guiding research question is thus whether DINOv3 features, together
with a class-anchor objective adapted to the one-class setting, constitute an effective basis
for anomaly detection and localisation on brain MRI, and, where they do not, what the
underlying reasons are.

   The contributions of this work are the following:

Background

self-supervised representation learning and the DINOv3 model that provides our back-
bone. Section 2.2 surveys the main paradigms of unsupervised anomaly detection. Sec-
tion 2.3 presents the Class Anchor Margin loss in its original supervised form. Section 2.4

2.1     Self-Supervised Representation Learning and DI-
        NOv3
    Self-supervised learning (SSL) trains representations directly from unlabelled data
by solving pretext tasks that require no human annotation. In computer vision this is
particularly attractive, since it removes the dependence on large labelled datasets and
allows training to scale to very large image collections. The representations learned this
way have repeatedly been shown to transfer well to downstream tasks, often rivalling or
surpassing features learned under full supervision [9, 10].
    The DINO family of methods learns such representations through a form of self-
distillation, in which a student network is trained to match the output of a teacher network
built as an exponential moving average of the student, using different augmented views
of the same image and no labels at all [11]. A notable property of these models is the
emergence of semantically meaningful structure in their features and attention maps.
Subsequent work scaled both the model and the training data substantially, producing
general-purpose features suitable for a broad range of tasks without fine-tuning [12].
    DINOv3 [1] continues this line of work and is the backbone used here. It is a Vision
Transformer (ViT) [13] trained at large scale on a curated collection of images. For an
input image, the model produces a global representation in the form of a class (CLS) to-


                                              9
ken and a set of patch tokens, one per image patch, that constitute a dense feature map;
it additionally uses a small number of register tokens that absorb global information and
reduce artefacts in the patch features. A central finding reported for DINOv3 is that
its dense features match or exceed specialised and supervised models on dense predic-
tion tasks such as segmentation, depth estimation, and correspondence, which makes the
backbone a strong general-purpose feature extractor.
    Two aspects of DINOv3 are directly relevant to this work. First, the authors observe
that dense patch-level features require care during training at large scale: the consistency
of the patch feature map may deteriorate even while global benchmarks keep improv-
ing. They introduce a regularisation technique, termed Gram anchoring, that constrains
the structure of pairwise patch similarities toward that of an earlier checkpoint, thereby
preserving local feature quality [1]. This observation motivates the comparison between
frozen-feature scoring and trained anchor variants in the present study. Second, for down-
stream use the authors recommend applying the final layer normalisation, which is trained
to suppress a small number of high-magnitude feature dimensions; this informs our choice
to use last-layer features with the register tokens excluded.
    For medical anomaly detection, a backbone of this kind is appealing precisely because
annotated medical data are scarce: strong, transferable features can be obtained without
task-specific supervision. At the same time, the features are learned on natural images,
so a degree of domain shift relative to medical imaging is expected, a point we return to
in the analysis [9, 10].


2.2     Paradigms of Unsupervised Anomaly Detection
    Unsupervised, or one-class, anomaly detection assumes that only normal samples are
available at training time. The model learns a representation or a generative account of
the normal data, and at inference assigns each sample an anomaly score that measures its
deviation from this learned notion of normality. Existing methods are commonly grouped
into two families: reconstruction-based and feature-based (also called projection-based)
approaches [4, 14, 6, 5].


2.2.1    Reconstruction-Based Methods
    Reconstruction-based methods train a generative model, such as an autoencoder, a
variational autoencoder, a generative adversarial network, or, more recently, a diffusion
model, to reconstruct normal images. The underlying assumption is that a model trained
only on normal data will reconstruct normal inputs accurately but fail to reconstruct
anomalous regions, so that the reconstruction error serves as an anomaly score and sup-
ports pixel-level localisation. A recurring diﬀiculty is that a suﬀiciently expressive gen-


                                            10
erative model may generalise well enough to reconstruct anomalies too, which weakens
the contrast between normal and abnormal reconstruction errors [5, 15]. Various regu-
larisation strategies, including denoising, masking, and memory mechanisms, have been
proposed to limit this effect.


2.2.2    Feature-Based Methods
    Feature-based methods instead map images into an embedding space, typically using a
pretrained network, and detect anomalies by reasoning about the geometry or distribution
of the resulting features. Several sub-families exist. One-class classification methods, such
as one-class support vector machines and Deep SVDD, seek a compact region, for instance
a minimum-enclosing hypersphere, that contains the normal features, and treat distance
from this region as the anomaly score [7, 16]. Memory-bank methods, such as PaDiM
and PatchCore, store representative normal patch features and score a test patch by
its distance to the nearest stored features, which makes them effective for fine-grained
localisation [17, 3]. Teacher–student or knowledge-distillation methods detect anomalies
through the discrepancy between a teacher and a student network that was trained to
imitate it only on normal data [18]. Normalising-flow methods explicitly model the density
of normal features and flag low-likelihood samples as anomalous.
    Empirically, feature-based methods tend to outperform reconstruction-based ones on
medical benchmarks, in part because they reason about high-level structure rather than
pixel-level fidelity [5, 14]. This observation directly motivates the design choices in this
work, which is primarily feature-based, and informs the inclusion of a memory-bank base-
line.
    A further practical consideration is how patch-level scores are aggregated into an
image-level decision. Because an anomaly often occupies only a small fraction of an image,
averaging scores over all locations dilutes the signal; strong methods instead aggregate
using the maximum or the mean of the highest-scoring locations.


2.3     The Class Anchor Margin Loss
    Objective functions for learning discriminative embeddings are often divided into
statistical-learning losses, such as cross-entropy, and metric-learning losses, such as the

require mining pairs or tuples of examples [2]. The Class Anchor Margin (CAM) loss [2]
was proposed for supervised, multi-class content-based image retrieval as a metric-learning
objective that avoids pair mining by expressing the loss with respect to a set of learnable
class anchors.

revisited when the objective is transferred to the one-class setting.


2.4     The BMAD Brain MRI Benchmark and Evalua-
        tion Metrics
    BMAD [5] is a benchmark for medical anomaly detection that reorganises existing
datasets from several imaging domains into a standardised one-class setting, with training
sets composed only of normal images. This work uses its brain MRI subset, which is
derived from the FLAIR modality of the BraTS2021 brain tumour segmentation dataset
[8]. Three-dimensional volumes are sliced along the axial plane within a fixed depth range,
and slices are labelled according to the tumour segmentation: slices without tumour are
treated as normal and slices containing tumour as anomalous. The local split used in this


                                              12
thesis provides 7,500 normal training slices, a validation set of 39 normal and 44 anomalous
slices, and a test set of 3,715 slices composed of 640 normal and 3,075 anomalous samples.
All slices have a resolution of 240 × 240 pixels, and anomalous slices include segmentation
masks that enable pixel-level evaluation [5].
    Anomaly detection is assessed at two levels. Image-level performance is measured
by the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) computed over
per-image anomaly scores [19]. Precision-recall metrics are also useful under class im-
balance [20, 21], and pixel-level localisation is measured by the AUROC computed over
per-pixel scores. The benchmark also reports the per-region overlap (PRO), a threshold-
independent localisation metric that averages overlap over individual connected anomaly
components and therefore weights small and large anomalies more equally [5]. The bench-
mark authors note that pixel-level AUROC should be interpreted together with qualitative
maps and, when available, region-level metrics: in brain MRI, the uniform background
can make pixel classification easier than precise lesion delineation. Pixel AUROC remains
useful, and the BMAD discussion motivates care when using it as the only localisation
measure. Among the methods evaluated on this benchmark, feature-based approaches
such as PatchCore and reverse distillation achieve strong results, providing useful refer-
ence points for the present method [5, 3, 18].


2.5     Objectives in Context
    The preceding sections allow the objectives of this work to be stated precisely. DINOv3
provides strong, general-purpose dense features, while the Class Anchor Margin loss offers
a mechanism for organising an embedding space around anchors. The two components
are attractive together, but they originate from different settings: CAM was designed
for a supervised, multi-class problem in which the repeller separates genuinely distinct
classes, whereas medical anomaly detection is a one-class problem in which only normal
data are available and any subdivisions of the normal class are obtained algorithmically.
    The objective of this work is therefore to investigate whether, and to what extent, the
class-anchor mechanism can be adapted to one-class anomaly detection on top of DINOv3
features, by replacing class labels with cluster assignments obtained from the normal
data and by combining the resulting anchor-based score with auxiliary reconstruction-
and divergence-based scores. The study measures the resulting detection and localisation
performance on the BMAD brain MRI benchmark, compares it against a simple training-
free PatchCore baseline built on frozen DINOv3 features, and analyses the mechanisms
that govern its behaviour. The method that operationalises these objectives is presented
in the next chapter.

Proposed Method

    This chapter presents the method investigated in this work. The central idea is to
adapt a supervised class-anchor objective to a one-class anomaly detection problem. In
the original Class Anchor Margin loss, anchors correspond to semantic classes and the
loss learns compact, separated class clusters. In the present setting only normal brain
MRI slices are available during training. Consequently, anchors cannot represent disease
categories; instead, they represent prototypes of the normal feature distribution, obtained
by clustering DINOv3 features extracted from normal training images.
    The method is an empirical adaptation of CAM to a one-class setting. The design
choices described below were introduced to answer a specific question: can DINOv3 fea-
tures be organised around normal anchors in such a way that tumour-containing slices
become distant from the learned normal manifold?

3.2     DINOv3 Feature Extraction
    The backbone is a DINOv3 Vision Transformer [13, 1]. Unless otherwise stated, the
main experiments use the ViT-S/16 DINOv3 checkpoint; a smaller set of ablations also
tests ViT-B/16 variants. For an input image, the model produces a global class token and
a grid of patch tokens. The global variant of the method uses the CLS token as the image
representation, while the patch variant uses the dense patch-token grid. Register tokens
are excluded from the dense representation, following the intended use of DINO-style ViT
features for downstream dense tasks.
    In the global setting, the backbone representation is passed through a projection
bottleneck,
                                      𝑧 = 𝑔𝜙 (𝑓𝜃 (𝑥)),                              (3.1)

where 𝑓𝜃 is the DINOv3 backbone and 𝑔𝜙 is a learned projection layer. The projected
embedding 𝑧 is the space in which anchor distances are computed. In the patch setting,
the same principle is applied locally: each spatial token is compared with patch-level
normal anchors, producing a dense anomaly map before aggregation.


3.3     Anchor Construction
    Anchors are built from normal training data. First, features are extracted for normal
slices using the DINOv3 backbone. Then 𝑘-means clustering is applied to these nor-
mal features [22]. The resulting centroids, or the nearest real training samples to those
centroids depending on the experiment family, define the initial normal anchors.
    For 𝐾 anchors, let
                                  𝐶 = {𝑐1 , 𝑐2 , … , 𝑐𝐾 }                            (3.2)

be the set of anchor vectors. During training, each normal sample is assigned to its nearest
anchor or to the cluster determined during anchor preparation. At inference time, the

main image-level anomaly score is the distance to the nearest anchor,

should lie near at least one normal prototype, whereas an anomalous sample should be
farther from all of them.

3.5     Two-Stage Training
   The trained anchor method uses a two-stage procedure.


3.5.1    Stage 1: Anchor-Based Training
   Stage 1 trains the embedding space for anchor scoring. Normal images are passed
through DINOv3 and the projection bottleneck, and the adapted anchor objective en-


                                               16
courages their embeddings to approach the selected normal anchors. Depending on the
experiment family, the DINOv3 backbone is either frozen or trainable. This distinc-
tion is important because the experiments compare two complementary uses of DINOv3:
preserving its pretrained feature geometry and adapting that geometry through anchor
supervision.
    The output of Stage 1 is an anchor-based anomaly detector. At inference time, an
image receives a score equal to its distance from the nearest normal anchor.


3.5.2    Stage 2: Reconstruction and Divergence Signals
    Stage 2 adds a reconstruction branch. A decoder is trained to reconstruct normal im-
ages from the learned representation. The motivation is standard in anomaly detection: if
the decoder has learned only normal anatomy, anomalous structures may be reconstructed
poorly, leading to high reconstruction error [15].
    This stage produces two auxiliary signals. The first is a reconstruction score,

                                                        ̂
                                     𝑠recon (𝑥) = ‖𝑥 − 𝑥‖,                              (3.6)

computed either globally or through pixel-level reconstruction error. The second is a diver-
gence score that compares representations before and after the Stage-2 adaptation. The
intended interpretation is that anomalous images should induce larger representational
disagreement.
    These auxiliary signals play different roles in the experiments. Reconstruction provides
useful pixel-level evidence and can improve fused scores, while divergence is a more indirect
signal whose contribution depends strongly on the configuration. Chapter 4 reports their
empirical contribution, and Chapter 5 analyses why they behave differently.


3.6     Score Fusion
   When all signals are available, they are normalised and combined through late fusion:

                    𝑠fused (𝑥) = 𝑤𝑎 𝑠anchor (𝑥) + 𝑤𝑑 𝑠div (𝑥) + 𝑤𝑝 𝑠pixel (𝑥),          (3.7)

where 𝑤𝑎 , 𝑤𝑑 , and 𝑤𝑝 are fusion weights. In the main global centroid-anchor and patch-
anchor families, the default weights are 0.4, 0.3, and 0.3 for the anchor, divergence, and
pixel/reconstruction-derived signals. The experiments also report individual scores so
that the contribution of each branch can be interpreted separately from the fused score.
   Fusion is evaluated empirically, because each branch can contribute differently after
normalisation. A weak or anti-correlated branch can dilute the strongest score, so the
experiments report both individual signals and fused performance.

                                               17
3.7     Patch and Location-Aware Variants
    The global centroid-anchor model compares whole-image embeddings to global an-
chors. However, local anomalies motivate a patch-level variant of the global centroid-
anchor model in order to better capture those localities. The shared patch-anchor model
moves the same idea to the dense token grid while still using anchors prepared from
whole-image clustering. Each patch token is compared with normal patch anchors, and
the resulting dense distance map is aggregated into an image-level score.
    The first patch variant uses a shared patch-anchor bank. Later experiments intro-
duce a location-aware patch-centroid model, where patch features are clustered by spatial
position. The motivation is anatomical: a normal patch from one brain region is not nec-
essarily a suitable reference for another region. Location-aware anchors therefore preserve
some spatial context while retaining the feature-distance principle.
    This evolution leads to the main methodological arc of the thesis. We first tested
the core idea of global normal anchors, then we added reconstruction and fusion, then
turned toward local patch evidence, and finally tested whether location-specific normal
references improve the patch model. The results show that this evolution improves the
conceptual alignment with the medical problem and yields competitive trained variants,
while the frozen-feature PatchCore baseline remains the strongest reference point in this
experiment set.

Experimental Setup and Results

   This chapter reports the experiments conducted on the BMAD brain MRI bench-
mark. It identifies the best-performing configurations and analyses how each design
choice changes the behaviour of the system. For this reason, the results are presented
as a controlled evolution of method families: early anchor initialisation strategies, the
global centroid-anchor model, the shared patch-anchor model, the location-aware patch-
centroid model, and finally the frozen-feature PatchCore comparison.


4.1     Dataset and Evaluation Protocol
    All experiments use the brain MRI subset of BMAD, derived from BraTS2021 FLAIR
volumes. The local dataset used in this work contains 7,500 normal training slices. The
validation set contains 39 normal and 44 anomalous slices. The test set contains 640
normal and 3,075 anomalous slices, for a total of 3,715 test images. All slices have
resolution 240 × 240 pixels, and anomalous slices are accompanied by segmentation masks
for pixel-level evaluation.
    The main image-level metric is the area under the receiver operating characteristic
curve (AUROC), computed from per-image anomaly scores [19]. The area under the
precision-recall curve (AUPR) is also reported where useful, since precision-recall curves
provide complementary information under skewed class distributions [20, 21]. Pixel-level
localisation is evaluated with pixel AUROC and pixel AUPR. Following the BMAD bench-
mark discussion, pixel AUROC is interpreted together with the qualitative maps: in brain
MRI, the large uniform background can make pixel classification easier than precise le-
sion delineation. PRO would add a useful region-level view of localisation, but it was not
computed for the experiment set reported here.
    The default backbone for the final structured experiments is DINOv3 ViT-S/16. Ad-
ditional backbone and projection-dimension ablations were also run, including ViT-B/16
variants with larger projection layers. These runs are used to check whether the main
conclusions are sensitive to backbone capacity, while the main reported families remain

                                           19
the ViT-S/16 global, shared patch, and location-aware models. Training was performed
on a local workstation with an NVIDIA RTX 5070 Ti GPU and 32GB of RAM. In terms
of time, the overall experiment set runtime was approximately 180 hours.
    The main trained families follow the same two-stage protocol. Stage 1 trains the
anchor-scoring model for up to 100 epochs with batch size 64, learning rate 10−4 , weight
decay 10−4 , mixed precision, and validation after every epoch. Early stopping uses a
patience of 10 epochs; in the global centroid-anchor family a minimum of 20 epochs is
enforced before early stopping, whereas the patch families allow earlier stopping. Stage 2
is enabled for 70 epochs and trains the reconstruction/divergence heads while keeping the
encoder, anchors, anchor targets, and bottleneck frozen. It uses an MSE reconstruction
loss, cosine consistency, top-95-percentile aggregation for pixel-derived image scores, and
late fusion with weights 0.4 for the anchor score, 0.3 for divergence, and 0.3 for the
pixel/reconstruction score.

Chapter 5

Analysis and Discussion

    The results in Chapter 4 show a consistent pattern. DINOv3 features are highly useful
for anomaly detection, and the class-anchor models provide informative trained variants
with meaningful anchor, reconstruction, fusion, and patch-based ablations. At the same
time, the frozen-feature PatchCore baseline remains the strongest result in this experiment
set. This chapter analyses that outcome by examining how the CAM objective changes
when it is adapted from supervised multi-class retrieval to one-class medical anomaly
detection.


5.1     The Frozen-Feature Baseline as a Reference Point
    The strongest result in the project is obtained by PatchCore applied to frozen DINOv3
features. This is consistent with the motivation of DINOv3 itself: the model is designed
to provide strong general-purpose dense features without task-specific fine-tuning. For
anomaly detection, especially patch-level anomaly detection, this is a favourable match.
PatchCore preserves the pretrained feature geometry and performs nearest-neighbour
comparison against normal patch features.
    The trained anchor variants modify this representation. When the backbone is train-
able, the loss reshapes the DINOv3 space according to normal-data anchor assignments.
Since the available training data contains only normal slices, the objective can enforce com-
pactness and anchor structure on normal examples, but it cannot directly learn tumour-
specific separation. This helps explain why preserving pretrained patch geometry is a
strong reference point for the trained variants.
    This explains the central empirical contrast: frozen DINOv3 plus a simple memory
bank reaches 0.8837 image AUROC, while the main trained anchor families reach fused
image AUROC values around 0.83. The result suggests that, for this problem, the pre-
trained dense feature geometry is already highly informative, and that learned anchor
geometry must preserve this information to improve on it.


                                             27
5.2     How the CAM Repeller Changes in One Class
    The Class Anchor Margin loss was designed for supervised multi-class retrieval. In
that setting, the repeller term has a clear role: it pushes anchors of different semantic
classes apart. This increases inter-class separation and improves retrieval.
    In the BMAD one-class setting, the situation is different. All training images are
normal. When 𝐾 > 1, the anchors correspond to clusters within the normal distribution
instead of true semantic classes. These clusters may capture slice position, acquisition
variation, anatomical variation, or simply arbitrary partitions introduced by 𝑘-means.
Repelling these anchors therefore pushes apart regions that all belong to the same normal
class.
    This gives a plausible mechanism for the observed 𝐾 behaviour. With 𝐾 = 1, there
is no inter-anchor repeller and the model behaves like a compact one-centre description
of normality. With mid-range 𝐾, the repeller can create extra separation inside the
normal manifold, producing regions between normal clusters that are less well covered by
anchors. A normal test slice that falls into such a region can receive a higher anomaly
score. With very large 𝐾, the anchors become dense enough that nearest-anchor scoring
partly resembles a memory-bank method, so performance can recover through coverage.
    Thus, the 𝐾 sweep should not be interpreted as simply searching for an optimal
number of clusters. It shows that a term designed to separate real semantic classes has a
different effect when the “classes” are artificial subdivisions of normal anatomy.


5.3     Fixed Cluster Assignments Under a Moving En-
        coder
    Another diﬀiculty is the interaction between cluster assignments and a trainable en-
coder. The assignments are derived from initial DINOv3 features, but the embedding
space can change during training. Even when anchor vectors are learnable and move
with the optimisation, the original assignment of each normal sample to a cluster may no
longer describe the current feature geometry.
    This is therefore better understood as a stale-assignment problem than as a strictly
fixed-centroid problem. The model continues to enforce attraction according to pseudo-
labels defined by an earlier representation. In multi-class supervised learning, labels
remain meaningful even as the representation changes. In this one-class clustered setting,
however, the pseudo-labels are geometric artefacts. If the geometry drifts, the assignments
may become less meaningful over time.
    This observation supports two future directions: keep the DINOv3 backbone frozen
and train only a light scoring layer, or periodically recompute assignments and normal
clusters if anchor training is retained.

                                            28
5.4     The Role of Reconstruction
    The reconstruction branch was introduced to provide pixel-level maps and an auxiliary
image-level score. It proves useful in this role: the qualitative heatmaps show localisation
cues, pixel AUROC is often high, and reconstruction contributes to the fused scores in the
patch and location-aware families. However, reconstruction is not the dominant image-
level signal.
    There are two reasons for this. First, reconstruction-based anomaly detection is known
to be diﬀicult in medical imaging because a suﬀiciently expressive decoder can reconstruct
abnormal structures or produce errors dominated by anatomy rather than pathology [15].
Second, the decoder in this project reconstructs from semantic DINOv3 representations.
These features are excellent for describing image content, but they are not designed to
preserve every pixel-level detail needed for faithful reconstruction.
    As a result, reconstruction error can highlight tumours, but it also responds to edges,
contrast changes, slice complexity, and anatomical structures. This explains why recon-
struction improves some fused results and supports localisation, while the anchor score
remains the stronger direct image-level detector. It also explains why pixel AUROC is
interpreted together with visual maps rather than as the sole measure of localisation
quality.


5.5     Divergence Is an Unstable Auxiliary Signal
    The divergence score was intended to measure disagreement between representations
before and after Stage 2. In practice, it is often weak or even anti-correlated with anomaly
labels. For example, in the patch run reported in Chapter 4, the divergence AUROC is
below chance, while the anchor and reconstruction-related scores remain useful.
    The limitation is conceptual. The divergence branch compares changes induced by
the transition from Stage 1 to Stage 2. Both stages are trained on normal data, so
the disagreement signal is only indirectly related to pathology. Divergence may capture
representation drift, reconstruction diﬀiculty, or differences between branches, and these
effects do not consistently align with anomaly labels.
    This is why fusion is reported alongside the individual branches. Adding a weak
or anti-correlated signal to a strong signal can reduce performance after normalisation.
The main patch and location-aware families therefore support a measured interpretation:
reconstruction-derived signals are useful, while divergence is less consistent.




                                            29
5.6     Patch Anchors and the Locality of Tumours
    Patch-anchor scoring is conceptually better aligned with the medical task than global
CLS scoring. A tumour is usually a local abnormality; it may not dominate the global
representation of the slice. Dense patch features preserve spatial evidence and allow the
model to compare local regions against normal local references.
    The patch experiments support this reasoning with slightly improved gains in the final
structured families. The best shared patch run improves fused AUROC relative to the
global centroid-anchor family, and location-aware patch centroids slightly improve the
fused and pixel metrics. The raw image AUROC is lower than the best early exploratory
anchor runs, while the fused patch results are the strongest among the final structured
families.
    This suggests that locality is necessary but not suﬀicient. Patch-level scoring works
best when it preserves the pretrained patch feature geometry and uses a strong nearest-
neighbour memory mechanism. The learned anchor variants move in that direction, but
their training and fusion machinery introduce additional sources of instability.


5.7     Comparison With Existing Approaches
    The BMAD benchmark reports strong performance for feature-based anomaly detec-
tion methods such as PatchCore and reverse distillation. This project confirms the same
broad lesson with a DINOv3 backbone: feature-based frozen or lightly adapted methods
are very competitive, while reconstruction-heavy branches are less reliable [5, 14, 6, 15].
    The proposed anchor method has two advantages. It is interpretable at the level of
prototypes, and it provides a controlled way to study how a supervised metric-learning
loss behaves when transferred to one-class anomaly detection. Its main challenge is that
the class-anchor assumptions change in this new setting. In particular, the repeller term
and pseudo-classes must be interpreted as geometric structure inside the normal class
rather than as true semantic class separation.
    Although PatchCore remains stronger on this benchmark, this work contributes a sys-
tematic empirical analysis of CAM-style one-class anchor learning with DINOv3 features,
including the mechanisms that make PatchCore stronger in this experiment set and the
conditions under which anchor, reconstruction, and patch-based signals remain useful.


5.8     Limitations
   The interpretation of the results has several boundaries. First, the evaluation does
not include PRO. BMAD recommends this metric for localisation in domains with large
uniform backgrounds, so it remains a useful future addition. Second, the validation set


                                            30
is small, making fusion-weight selection noisy. Third, the experiments are conducted on
two-dimensional slices, leaving inter-slice context for future work.
    Despite these limitations, the experiments are suﬀicient to support the main con-
clusion: DINOv3 features are strong for this task, and adapting class-anchor training
to a one-class normal manifold requires care because the meaning of anchor separation
changes.


5.9     Practical Recommendations
    The analysis suggests several directions for future work. DINOv3 is a strong starting
point as a dense feature extractor, so future anchor methods should preserve as much of
its pretrained patch geometry as possible while adding task-specific structure only where
it is empirically helpful. If multiple normal prototypes are used, they should primarily
improve coverage of the normal manifold and avoid artificial separation that fragments
it. Patch-level memory or nearest-neighbour scoring remains a strong design choice for
localisation, and region-level metrics such as PRO would strengthen future evaluations.
If anchor learning is retained, promising variants include repeller-free objectives, periodic
reclustering, frozen-backbone scoring, and location-aware normal references.

Conclusions and Future Work

    This thesis investigated whether the Class Anchor Margin loss, originally designed
for supervised multi-class image retrieval, can be adapted to one-class medical anomaly
detection on brain MRI when combined with DINOv3 features. The proposed system used
normal-data anchors obtained through 𝑘-means clustering, a DINOv3 backbone with a
projection bottleneck, and a two-stage training procedure that produced anchor-based,
reconstruction-based, and divergence-based anomaly scores.
    The experimental results show that the adapted anchor method can detect anomalies
and can produce useful pixel-level heatmaps. The final structured families reach fused
AUROC values around 0.83 with pixel AUROC around 0.93, with the location-aware
patch-centroid variant obtaining 0.8295 fused image AUROC and 0.9350 pixel AUROC.
The strongest result in the project is obtained by a simpler frozen-feature PatchCore
baseline using DINOv3 features, which reaches 0.8837 image AUROC and 0.9612 pixel
AUROC.
    The main conclusion is analytical: DINOv3 features are highly valuable for brain MRI
anomaly detection, and class-anchor learning provides an interpretable way to organise
normal-data prototypes. The experiments also show that the assumptions of CAM change
in the one-class setting. In particular, the repeller term is meaningful when anchors
correspond to different semantic classes, but in this work it separates artificial clusters
of normal anatomy. This can reshape the normal manifold in ways that are not always
useful for anomaly detection. Similarly, trainable-backbone variants must preserve the
dense-feature quality that makes DINOv3 attractive in the first place.
    The reconstruction and divergence branches provide additional insight. Reconstruc-
tion supports localisation and contributes to the fused scores, even though its image-level
signal is not always as direct as the anchor score. Divergence is less consistent because
representation disagreement is only indirectly related to abnormality. These results show
why reporting individual branches alongside fusion is important: auxiliary signals can
help, but their contribution depends on calibration and on the experiment family.
    The broader lesson is that mixed experimental results can be scientifically useful when

                                            32
their mechanisms are understood. This work shows that transferring a supervised metric-
learning loss to one-class medical anomaly detection is not a matter of replacing class
labels with clusters. The geometry and meaning of the anchors change, and the objective
must change with them.
    Future work should therefore focus on preserving the strengths identified in the exper-
iments while removing the mismatched assumptions. Promising directions include frozen-
backbone anchor scoring, repeller-free one-class objectives, patch-level nearest-neighbour
memory banks, location-aware normal references, periodic reclustering when the repre-
sentation is trained, and evaluation with PRO to better measure lesion-level localisation.
A three-dimensional extension that uses inter-slice context would also be a natural next
step for brain MRI.
    In summary, this work demonstrates that self-supervised dense features are a strong
foundation for medical anomaly detection, and that class-anchor training is a promising
but delicate mechanism whose one-class adaptation requires careful design of anchors,
objectives, and fusion signals.
